# Dallas Willard (1935–2013)

## Biography

Dallas Albert Willard was an American philosopher and Christian spiritual formation teacher. Born September 4, 1935, in Buffalo, Missouri, he became one of the most influential voices in Protestant spiritual formation in the late 20th and early 21st centuries.

### Education
- **1956:** B.A. in Psychology from Tennessee Temple College
- **1957:** B.A. in Philosophy and Religion from Baylor University
- **1964:** Ph.D. in Philosophy from University of Wisconsin–Madison
  - Dissertation: "Meaning and Universals in Husserl's 'Logische Untersuchungen'"
  - Minor in History of Science

### Academic Career
- **1960–1965:** University of Wisconsin–Madison (research assistant, fellow, instructor)
- **1965–2013:** University of Southern California (USC) — 48 years
  - Assistant Professor (1965–1969)
  - Associate Professor (1969–1984)
  - Full Professor (1984–2013)
  - Director of School of Philosophy (1982–1985)

### Philosophy Background
Willard was an internationally recognized authority on **Edmund Husserl** and phenomenology:
- Translated many of Husserl's early writings into English for the first time
- Published extensively on epistemology, philosophy of mind, and logic
- Combined rigorous academic philosophy with practical spiritual formation

### Death
Dallas Willard died May 8, 2013, in Pasadena, California, from pancreatic cancer, at age 77.

## Core Contributions to Spiritual Formation

### 1. The Divine Conspiracy Framework
Willard's most influential book, *The Divine Conspiracy* (1998), presents the Christian life as:
- **A conspiracy:** God is conspiring to draw humanity into relationship and transformation
- **Hidden in plain sight:** The kingdom of God is available now, not just in heaven
- **Accessible to all:** Through Jesus, the "with-God" life is possible for everyone

### 2. Apprenticeship to Jesus
Willard reframed discipleship as **apprenticeship**:
- A disciple is "one who is with Jesus, learning to be like him"
- Being with Jesus precedes doing for Jesus
- The goal is Christlike character, not just correct belief or behavior

> "The key to authentic transformation is becoming an apprentice of Jesus—being with him, learning from him, becoming like him."

### 3. Training vs. Trying
One of Willard's most liberating insights:
- **Trying:** Mustering willpower to change (which doesn't work)
- **Training:** Engaging in practices that transform us over time

> "Grace is not opposed to effort [which is action], but to earning [which is attitude]."

### 4. The Renovation of the Heart
Willard saw transformation happening at the level of the **heart** (the inner person):
- The heart includes thoughts, feelings, choices, and social relations
- True change happens when the heart is transformed, not just behavior modified
- Spiritual disciplines are the means of this renovation

### 5. The VIM Pattern
Willard taught that spiritual formation requires three elements:
- **Vision:** Seeing the kingdom of God and life with God as desirable
- **Intention:** Deciding to be a disciple/apprentice of Jesus
- **Means:** Engaging the practices that enable transformation

### 6. The With-God Life
Willard emphasized that we are invited to live **with God** in all of life:
- Not just devotional times, but all moments
- Not just sacred activities, but ordinary work and relationships
- The "practice of the presence of God" (echoing Brother Lawrence)

## Key Works

### *The Divine Conspiracy* (1998)
- Christianity Today's Book of the Year (1999)
- Core thesis: The kingdom of God is available now through Jesus
- Addresses: How to live as Jesus would live if he were in our place

### *The Spirit of the Disciplines* (1988)
- Explains how spiritual disciplines enable transformation
- Key distinction: Disciplines are training, not performance
- Practical guidance on practicing disciplines

### *Renovation of the Heart* (2002)
- Christianity Today Book Award (2003)
- Addresses the transformation of the whole person
- Structure: Six aspects of human personality (thoughts, feelings, will, body, social context, soul)

### *Hearing God* (originally *In Search of Guidance*, 1984)
- On developing a conversational relationship with God
- Addresses discernment, hearing God's voice, and guidance

### *Knowing Christ Today* (2009)
- On the knowledge of Christ as the foundation for Christian life
- Addresses epistemology and Christian knowledge claims

## Spiritual Disciplines (The Means of Grace)

Willard organized disciplines into categories:

### Disciplines of Abstinence (Renunciation)
- Solitude
- Silence
- Fasting
- Frugality
- Chastity
- Secrecy (doing good deeds in secret)
- Sacrifice

### Disciplines of Engagement (Activity)
- Study
- Worship
- Celebration
- Service
- Prayer
- Fellowship
- Confession
- Submission

**Key principle:** Disciplines are **means**, not ends. They create space for God to transform us.

## Important Distinctions

### Passivity vs. Activity
Willard critiqued both:
- **Passive approaches:** Waiting for God to change us without engagement
- **Works-based approaches:** Trying to earn transformation through effort

The third way: **Active training** empowered by grace.

### External vs. Internal Change
Willard warned against:
- Changing external behavior without internal transformation
- "Sin management" rather than heart renovation
- Performance without relationship

### Disciplines as Means, Not Merits
- Disciplines don't earn God's favor
- Disciplines create space for transformation
- Disciplines are training exercises, not legalistic requirements

## Connections to Other Influences

### With Bavinck
Both emphasize the **transformation of the whole person** and the integration of faith and life. Willard adds the practical "how" to Bavinck's systematic "what."

### With Tim Keller
Keller often cited Willard's influence on spiritual formation. Both emphasize that Christianity is about **relationship and transformation**, not just doctrine.

### Application for Biblical Counseling
Willard's framework helps Mel distinguish between:
- Behavior modification (external) vs. heart transformation (internal)
- Trying (willpower) vs. training (disciplines)
- Performance (earning) vs. apprenticeship (grace)

## Practical Takeaways for Mel's Ministry

1. **Always offer concrete practices** — not just principles
2. **Emphasize grace-powered effort** — not passivity, not earning
3. **Focus on the heart** — not just behavior
4. **Present the "with-God" life** as available now
5. **Use apprenticeship language** — being with Jesus, learning to be like him
6. **Encourage disciplines** as training, not performance
